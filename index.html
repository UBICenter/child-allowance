The impact of a child allowance on child povertyÂ¶

1 in 7 children in the US lives in poverty, raising stress and crime rates, worsening educational outcomes, and shrinking the economy by up to $1 trillion annually. Research shows that giving money to families with children, as most developed countries do, reduces each of these issues. A child allowance is a policy that gives families an equal amount for each child.

In this brief, we show the effects of child allowances on child poverty. Start with the interactive map below, which shows how various child allowance amounts would impact child poverty rates in each state across the country.

### CALCULATIONS ###

def ca_pov(state, age_group, ca_monthly=0):
    '''
    Calculates poverty rate from child allowance amount for a given state and
    age group.
    
    Args:
        state: State, or 'US' for national.
        age_group: Group to calculate poverty rate for:
            'child' filters to under-18 poverty
            'adult' filters to 18+ poverty
            any other value shows poverty for all ages
        ca_monthly: Monthly child allowance. Defaults to zero.
        
    Returns:
        Poverty rate.
    '''
    # Filter by state, unless US overall.
    if state == 'US':
        target_persons = person.copy(deep=True)    
    else:
        target_persons = person[person.state==state].copy(deep=True)
    # Filter by age group.
    if age_group == 'child':
        target_persons = target_persons[target_persons.child]
    if age_group == 'adult':
        target_persons = target_persons[target_persons.adult]
    # Calculate child allowance and associated SPM resources and poverty
    # status.
    target_persons['total_ca'] = (
        target_persons.total_children * ca_monthly * 12)
    target_persons['new_spm_resouces'] = (
        target_persons.total_ca + target_persons.spmtotres)
    target_persons['poor'] = (
        target_persons.new_spm_resouces < target_persons.spmthresh)
    target_pop = (target_persons.weight).sum()
    total_poor = (target_persons.weight * target_persons.poor).sum()
    # Return it as a 0-100 percentage.
    return (total_poor / target_pop * 100).round(1)

def pov_row(row):
    ''' Calculates the poverty rate associated with attributes in a row.
    
    Args:
        row: Row in a pandas DataFrame with variables for state, age_group,
            and ca_monthly.
    
    Returns:
        Poverty rate (see ca_pov() function).
    '''
    return ca_pov(row.state, row.age_group, row.ca_monthly)


# Create a data frame of all necessary inputs.
states = person.state.unique().tolist()
summary = mdf.cartesian_product({'state': ['US'] + states,
                                 'ca_monthly': np.arange(0, 501, 25),
                                 'age_group': ['child', 'adult', 'all']})

# Apply the function across the data frame to calculate poverty rates.
summary['poverty_rate'] = summary.apply(pov_row, axis=1)
summary['geo_order'] = summary.state == 'US'
summary = summary.sort_values(['geo_order','state', 'ca_monthly'],
                              ascending=(True, True, True))

While a child allowance has the most direct impact on children, it also reduces adult poverty by lifting parents above the line. You can use the dropdown menu to explore how a child allowance impacts poverty across age demographics.

The closest thing America currently has to a child allowance is the Child Tax Credit, which provides up to $2,000 per child to eligible families. Yet more than one in three children in the U.S are ineligible for the full child tax credit because their families donât earn enough to qualify, and another one in ten are excluded because their families earn too much.

The American Family Act would expand the Child Tax Credit by giving $300 per month for children under 6 and $250 per month for children ages 6 to 16, phasing out for high-income families. These payments would lift 4.5 million children and 3.2 million adults out of poverty. It has 185 cosponsors in the House and 37 in the Senate, and is included in the HEROES Act.

These charts reflect the opportunity for federal legislation to reduce child poverty across states, as well as statesâ opportunity to reduce child poverty with child allowances of their own. Currently, six states have their own supplemental Child Tax credits, but several of these fail to reach the poorest children because they only offset positive state tax liability.

By ensuring the poorest children are reached, child allowances and fully refundable Child Tax Credits would have significant impacts on child poverty, well-being, and development.
